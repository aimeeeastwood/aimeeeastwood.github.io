Identity, Values, and Strengths Exploration

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

A long time ago, a friend asked me to be a witness in a child custody case, and asked me to lie. I refused, even though I knew it was important to her. The idea of lying in a legal setting is just a bridge too far for me. It ended our friendship which I don’t like, but looking back, I made the right decision.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

I was raised in quite a English-lite Anglican middle class family. Growing up, telling the truth was extremely important as was the idea of dignity. Later I would move to Japan for 8 years and strangely found the way I was raised made it quite easy to fit into Japanese society, where honour and integrity are also extremely important. So to this day, my values revolve around honesty, fairness, and living for the greater good.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

I am Agnostic, but it cannot be denied that religion has had a massive influence on me. According to a test I did, Spirituality comes out on top. I think this means that I often think of the “big picture” when it comes to moral questions and even though I don’t follow any religion, I believe that there is a “bigger picture”. I believe this can help me to think beyond what's immediately obvious and dwell on the unforeseen. Honesty and bravery are high too. I value truth above most other things, and I also value trust. In a workspace that means I “own up” when I make a mistake, and if I promise to do something, I am honour bound to follow through. To me, especially in the world these days, honesty is a strength, that you can be trusted. The other is perspective. Maybe because of my time in the humanities (history and journalism), I’ve learned to see things from as many perspectives as possible. That doesn’t mean agreeing with all perspectives, but it does mean hearing them out and giving them a chance. I believe that more information is always better, and one of the biggest roadblocks people come across when dealing with others is an inability to escape one's own perspective. Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development.

How might these affect your learning journey?

So, I am quite a “dark” gothy person, full admission. I am pretty sure I am neuro divergent. This combined with my values of honesty can cause problems, especially when dealing with people higher in the hierarchy than myself. I am also very chatty and easily distracted which can be extremely frustrating for my colleagues. Being organised and sticking to a schedule is also one of my weaknesses, though I do take steps to try and manage it, for example, always turning up early, using my phone's calendar and dividing my day into “need time” and “want time”. Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension.

Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

I think when a band I was in was recording an album, I wanted strings on a couple of tracks but the other band members were skeptical. Words alone were enough, so I made a demo with keyboards on my own. Then they started to come around. I think the lesson here is about perspective and communication. My bandmates couldn’t hear what I heard in my head. And to be fair, they couldn’t be faulted for not knowing. And in that situation talk is not enough. You have to show. And very often people might not be on the same page as you, but it’s not that they are disagreeing with you, they just don’t see your perspective.
